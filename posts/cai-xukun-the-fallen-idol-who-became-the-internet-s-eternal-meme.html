Cai Xukun – The Fallen Idol Who Became the Internet's Eternal Meme

Internet figures By Weilin Published: 2026-04-18 00:00
In the annals of Chinese pop culture, few names carry as much weight — or as many punchlines — as Cai Xukun, known to his fans as KUN. Born on August 2, 1998 in Wenzhou, Zhejiang, this singer, songwriter, and former NINE PERCENT captain has lived a career that reads like a tragedy in three acts: meteoric rise, cataclysmic fall, and a strange second life as the internet's favorite absurdist icon.

The Idol Factory

Cai‘s journey began early. At just 14, he auditioned for Upward! Youngsters, then landed bit roles in TV dramas like Fairy Tale of Two Halves. In 2016, he formally debuted with the boy band SWIN, but true stardom wouldn't arrive until 2018. That year, he entered Idol Producer, an auditions survival show, and crushed the competition, finishing first place with a staggering margin and becoming the center and captain of the supergroup NINE PERCENT. His debut EP *1* won Rookie of the Year at the Asian New Song Chart Awards.

King of the Charts

By 2020, Cai was untouchable. His single “Lover” shattered 15 music chart records and became a karaoke staple. He won Best Male Singer and Asian Popularity Artist at the 27th Oriental Music Awards, then returned to mentor the next generation of idols on Youth With You, Season 2. He graced GQ covers, performed sold-out shows, and seemed destined to define a generation.

The Meme That Changed Everything

Then came the basketball video. During a Idol Producer introduction, Cai performed a brief, stiff dance while bouncing a basketball to the song *“Just Because You’re So Beautiful.” The line quickly slurred into “Jī nǐ tài měi” — ”Chicken, you’re so beautiful.” The internet exploded. Clips of Cai‘s awkward moves were remixed into countless parodies across Bilibili and Douyin. ”Xiao Heizi” (”Little Blackies”), his haters, flooded comments with ”jī” puns and fake praise. His attempt to send legal letters to Bilibili only fueled the fire. Cai became the laughingstock of China’s internet — yet the memes have outlived any shame.

The Collapse

In 2023, a real scandal hit. A tabloid reported an alleged one-night stand and an abortion, causing a massive public backlash. Brands cut ties, and the reality show Keep Running removed him entirely. He vanished from China’s variety shows.

Ghost in the Machine

Since then, Cai has kept his head down. In 2025, he released the melancholic English single “Deadman,” a haunting reflection on love and ruin. He also won a defamation lawsuit against several gossip accounts. As of 2026, he occasionally surfaces for brand events — most notably when a stranger rushed onstage to hug him in Hangzhou, sparking fresh debate about fan safety.

Love him, laugh at him, or both — Cai Xukun has become something stranger than a pop star: a cultural scar, a punchline, and a ghost who won‘t stop making music. No rehearsal. No regrets. Just jī.
